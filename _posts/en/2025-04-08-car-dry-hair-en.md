When I was recently traveling, I forgot to dry my hair before leaving the hotel one day. It was a rainy day, and even after a few hours, my hair was still wet. I felt uncomfortable.  

I considered going to a nearby hotel, paying the staff a small fee, and asking to use a hairdryer. Alternatively, I could drive back to the hotel for half an hour just to dry my hair.  

Then, suddenly, I thought, "Why don’t I use the car’s air system to dry it?" I went to my car—a Neta electric car—turned on the heat, and set it to level 7. I lowered my head close to the vent. Soon, my hair was dry.

This became one of the happiest moments of my trip.

Using a car's air system—like the air conditioning or heater vents—to dry your hair isn’t inherently dangerous, but it’s not exactly practical or efficient either. The airflow from car vents is designed to regulate cabin temperature, not to blow hard enough to dry hair quickly. You’d probably be sitting there for a while, awkwardly holding your head near the vent, and the result might be uneven at best.  

If the air is hot, it could potentially damage your hair over time, like any heat source, but a one-off attempt isn’t likely to cause harm. The bigger issue is hygiene—car vents can harbor dust, pollen, or mold if the system isn’t well-maintained, and you’d be blasting that into your hair. Plus, if you’re driving, it’s a distraction, which could be risky.  

In short: it’s “okay” in that it won’t kill you, but it’s not a great idea. A towel or a proper hairdryer would do the job better and faster. Why are you in a spot where the car’s AC is your go-to, anyway?
