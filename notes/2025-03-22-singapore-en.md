## 1. Country Overview

Singapore is a sovereign city-state located at the southern tip of the Malay Peninsula in Southeast Asia. Despite its small geographic size, it has established itself as a major global hub for trade, finance, and transportation. Its strategic position along important shipping lanes has historically contributed to its success as a trading post and later as a key economic player in the region.

## 2. Economic Landscape and GDP Distribution

### Economic Structure

Singapore’s economy is notably diversified despite its limited natural resources. The country’s gross domestic product (GDP) is primarily driven by the following sectors:

- **Services Sector:**  
  This is the largest component of the economy, accounting for roughly 70% or more of the GDP. Key industries include:
  - **Financial Services:** Banking, insurance, and asset management.
  - **Trade & Logistics:** Given its strategic port, shipping and logistics play a significant role.
  - **Tourism & Hospitality:** With attractions ranging from cultural districts to modern architecture, tourism remains robust.
  - **Professional Services:** Legal, consulting, and other business services are well-developed.

- **Manufacturing Sector:**  
  Contributing about 20% of the GDP, Singapore’s manufacturing is highly advanced and focuses on:
  - **Electronics & Semiconductors:** The backbone of its industrial output.
  - **Biomedical Sciences:** With substantial investments in research and development.
  - **Chemicals and Precision Engineering:** These sectors cater to both local and international markets.

- **Other Sectors:**  
  The remainder of the economy includes construction, real estate, and niche agricultural activities—each playing a smaller but important role.

### Wealth and GDP per Capita

Singapore is among the world’s wealthiest nations by GDP per capita. Its high standard of living is supported by strong economic policies, a robust financial system, and consistent investments in infrastructure and human capital. The nation’s wealth is further bolstered by significant foreign investments and a business-friendly environment that attracts multinational corporations.

## 4. Historical Background

### Colonial Era and Early Development

- **Founding as a Trading Post:**  
  In 1819, Sir Stamford Raffles established Singapore as a British trading post. Its location made it a natural stopover for ships navigating the trade routes between the East and the West.
  
- **World War II and Japanese Occupation:**  
  During World War II, Singapore was occupied by Japanese forces—a period that left a lasting impact on its national psyche and later contributed to its strong emphasis on defense and self-reliance.

### Road to Independence

- **Post-War Developments:**  
  Following the war, Singapore underwent rapid changes in governance and economic policy. Its brief merger with Malaysia in 1963 and subsequent full independence in 1965 marked the beginning of a new era.

- **Transformation into a Modern Economy:**  
  Over the following decades, Singapore transitioned from a developing nation into a high-income, technologically advanced society. This transformation was driven by forward-looking policies, heavy investments in education, and a focus on industrialization and global trade.
